Power of Attorney Basics

Help without giving up control.

A Power of Attorney, or POA, lets a competent adult choose someone trusted to help with specific matters. The person signing it stays in charge, keeps their rights, and can limit or cancel the authority while they still have the ability to make decisions.

The main idea

This is not guardianship. It is not a court taking rights away. It is the mother choosing, on her own terms, who can help and what kind of help is allowed.

General information only: This guide is educational and is not legal advice. For advice about a specific situation, talk with a Washington state elder law attorney or visit Washington Law Help.

Key Terms

These words appear often in POA documents. Knowing them makes the paperwork less intimidating.

PrincipalThe person granting authority. In this guide, that is the mother.
AgentThe trusted helper. This can also be called attorney-in-fact.
DurableThe POA can keep working if the principal later cannot make decisions.
RevokeCancel or take back the POA while competent.
FiduciaryThe agent must act in the principal's best interest.

Start here

What a POA does

A POA lets the mother choose a trusted person to help with certain matters. It can be broad or narrow, depending on what she wants.

More detail

The mother can decide what the daughter may do, what she may not do, and whether the authority should be limited to one task or cover practical financial life more broadly.

Signing a POA does not mean the mother stops managing her own life. It simply gives someone else permission to help.

Important

What a POA is not

It is not the same as guardianship or conservatorship. No judge removes rights just because a POA is signed.

Why this matters

Guardianship and conservatorship involve a court deciding that someone cannot make decisions and appointing another person to decide for them.

A POA is different. The mother grants authority voluntarily, and she can take it back while she has decision-making ability.

Most practicalFinancial

Durable Financial Power of Attorney

This is usually the most useful document for bills, banking, insurance, Medicaid or Apple Health, taxes, agencies, pension paperwork, and records.

What it can cover
  • Pay bills, rent, utilities, and medical expenses.
  • Access and use bank accounts for the mother's benefit.
  • Deposit income, including pension payments and Social Security.
  • Manage insurance claims and Medicaid or Apple Health paperwork.
  • Communicate with DSHS, Social Security, the IRS, hospitals, clinics, and pension offices.
  • Obtain financial, medical, tax, and benefit records when needed for paperwork.
Why durable matters

"Durable" means the authority can continue if the mother's health later declines and she cannot manage things herself. A non-durable POA often stops at the exact moment help is most needed.

Narrow help

Limited or Special POA

A limited POA gives authority for one specific task or category, then expires or becomes irrelevant when that task is finished.

Example

The mother could sign a limited POA that says, "My daughter may submit and manage my Medicaid application and communicate with DSHS on my behalf."

That can be useful if she wants help with one thing but is not ready to authorize broader financial help.

Usually not enough

General POA, non-durable

A general non-durable POA may give broad authority, but it usually stops working if the principal becomes incapacitated.

Why it may not fit

For aging or health planning, this can be a problem. If the document stops working when the mother cannot manage things herself, it may disappear right when the daughter needs authority to help.

Separate documentMedical

Health Care POA

This is separate from financial POA. It names someone who can make medical decisions only if the mother cannot speak for herself.

When it matters

It can matter during hospitalization, medical emergencies, surgery, serious illness, or any time the mother cannot communicate her wishes.

While the mother can still make and communicate medical decisions, she remains in control of her own care.

Rights kept

What the mother keeps

Signing a POA does not mean giving up control, money, property, or the right to make decisions.

Rights to remember
  • She can keep managing her own finances and signing her own checks.
  • She can set limits on what the daughter is allowed to do.
  • She can ask for an accounting of how money was spent.
  • She can revoke the POA while she has the ability to do so.
  • She can name someone different later if circumstances change.
Daughter's duty

What the agent must do

The daughter does not own the mother's money or property. She is allowed to manage things only for the mother's benefit.

Fiduciary duty

The agent has a legal duty to act in the mother's best interest, follow the mother's wishes, keep accurate records, and avoid using the authority for personal gain.

Quick Comparison

These documents solve different problems. Many families need more than one.

Durable Financial POA

Covers: Bills, banking, taxes, Medicaid, insurance, agencies, pension, and records.

When: Usually immediately upon signature, depending on the document.

Limited POA

Covers: One specific task, form, agency, or category.

When: Immediately for that task only, unless the document says otherwise.

Health Care POA

Covers: Medical decisions only.

When: Only when the mother cannot speak for herself or communicate her wishes.
