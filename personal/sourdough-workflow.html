Directions

Five phases, from mix to slice. Tap a card to expand the full method.

1 Mix & Rest
  • Mix to shaggy dough
  • Rest 30–60 min

Combine water and active starter. Add flour and salt; mix until no dry flour remains. Cover and rest 30–60 min.

2 Strengthen the Dough
  • 4 fold sets
  • Space 30 min apart
  • Use damp hands

Perform one set of stretch-and-folds every 30 min for 2 hrs. Cover between sets.

3 Finish Bulk Fermentation
  • Let dough rise 30–50%
  • Look for bubbles and a slight jiggle
  • Total bulk: about 3–6 hrs

After the folds, leave covered until the dough is smoother, slightly domed, bubbly at the edges, and 30–50% larger. Count total bulk time from when the starter was added.

4 Shape & Final Proof
  • Shape with surface tension
  • Room proof: 1–3 hrs
  • Cold proof: 12–24 hrs

Turn onto a lightly floured surface and shape into a tight ball or loaf. Place seam-side up in a floured banneton.

Choose one:

Room temperature: Cover and proof until slightly puffy and a gentle press springs back slowly, about 1–3 hrs.
Cold proof: Cover and refrigerate 12–24 hrs.

5 Bake & Cool
  • Preheat Dutch oven 45–60 min
  • Covered: 20–25 min
  • Uncovered: 20–25 min
  • Cool at least 1 hr

Preheat the Dutch oven to 450–500°F (230–260°C). Turn the dough onto parchment, smooth-side up, and score. Bake covered for 20–25 min, then uncovered for 20–25 min, until deeply browned. Cool on a wire rack for at least 1 hr.

Reference & Assumptions

The thinking behind the numbers.

About hydration

Lower hydration (60–68%) is easier to shape and produces a tighter crumb. Higher hydration (75–85%) yields a more open, airy crumb but is harder to handle. It mostly affects dough feel and crumb structure rather than flavor.

About starter amount

Starter % is your inoculation level, with a common range of 10–30%. A higher percentage ferments faster. Because the starter contributes both flour and water, it raises your effective hydration above the entered value.

About salt

Salt is typically around 2%. It contributes flavor and slightly slows fermentation. Spoon-volume varies considerably by salt type — Diamond Crystal kosher is much less dense than table salt, so a teaspoon of it weighs roughly half as much.

Assumptions used in conversions

Volume readouts are rough guides built on these assumptions:

  • Bread flour ≈ 130 g per cup
  • Water: 1 g = 1 mL; 1 cup = 240 mL
  • Starter assumed 100% hydration, ≈ 240 g per cup as a rough approximation — active starter is airy, so this is loose
  • Salt density per teaspoon: table salt ≈ 6 g, Diamond Crystal kosher ≈ 2.8 g, Morton kosher ≈ 4.8 g

Weigh ingredients for best results — volumes are for quick reference only.
